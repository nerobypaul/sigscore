## PART 1: WHAT IS WORKING NOW FOR DEVTOOL COMPANIES

### The Playbook Behind the Winners

**Cursor -- $500M ARR with zero marketing spend**
Cursor reached $500M ARR in May 2025 with 40 employees and zero dollars in marketing. The entire engine was product-led word-of-mouth. The lesson: when the product genuinely makes developers faster, distribution happens through conversations.

Key mechanics:
- Free tier generous enough to create habit
- Individual adoption inside companies triggers team-wide upgrades
- Developer identity signal: "I use Cursor" became a status marker
- No content marketing, no launch events, no DevRel -- pure product gravity

**Supabase -- Launch Week as a distribution engine**
Supabase grew from 1M to 4.5M+ developers in under a year using content, community, and viral launch events. Their Launch Week format -- shipping one major feature per day for five consecutive days, every quarter -- became the most imitated marketing tactic in devtools (now on Launch Week 15).

What makes it work:
- Creates anticipation and FOMO (countdown pages, tickets)
- Bundled with hackathon that generates user content
- Each day's launch gets its own blog post, tweet thread, and HN submission
- The format itself became open-source (launchweek.dev)

**Linear -- $35K lifetime marketing spend, $400M valuation**
Linear didn't just build a project tracker -- they built a belief system: "speed is a feature, not a trade-off." $35K total marketing spend produced a valuation that dwarfs competitors spending millions. Mechanism: bottom-up adoption via one engineer.

**Resend -- Founder-as-brand + open source flywheel**
Zeno Rocha's personal brand (100K+ Twitter followers) was the primary acquisition channel. React Email as open source drove awareness for the paid API. Lesson: an OSS project developers genuinely need is the best top-of-funnel.

**Vercel -- Framework ownership as distribution**
Vercel owns Next.js. 100,000+ monthly signups driven by a framework that is the default for React apps. The "deploy in seconds" moment is the viral loop.

## PART 2: DEVELOPER MARKETING THAT BREAKS THROUGH

The pattern: **the marketing IS the product experience**. No gap between "learn about it" and "use it."

**Principles that work:**

1. **Opinionated positioning** -- Not "we're a better CRM" but "your current stack is blind to developer intent."
2. **Speed as proof** -- Connect GitHub/npm, see your first developer signal in under 60 seconds.
3. **Building in public** -- Share roadmaps, decisions, progress publicly.
4. **The "I switched" testimonial** -- One genuine tweet from a Head of Growth is worth more than any landing page.
5. **The demo IS the pitch** -- 60-second video: npm installs spike, DevSignal surfaces the company, enriches contacts, scores them, triggers Slack notification.

## PART 5: LESSONS FROM PLG CRM FAILURES

### The dead and pivoted

| Company | Raised | Status |
|---------|--------|--------|
| Calixa | $16.3M | Shut down Jan 2024 |
| Koala | $15M | Acqui-hired by Cursor, Sep 2025 |
| Endgame | $17M+ | Pivoted to AI enterprise sales |
| Correlated | -- | Shut down |

### Why they failed

1. **Required engineering buy-in** -- GTM teams couldn't self-serve setup. DevSignal's advantage: npm/PyPI/GitHub connectors pull PUBLIC data. No engineering needed.

2. **Tried to replace the CRM** -- DevSignal feeds INTO your existing CRM, doesn't replace it.

3. **Horizontal instead of vertical** -- "PLG CRM for everyone" is too broad. DevSignal is laser-focused on devtools.

4. **Couldn't justify standalone pricing** -- At $79-299/mo, DevSignal is a credit card expense, not a procurement cycle.

5. **Raised too much, too early** -- Stay lean. The price point works for bootstrapped economics.

**Critical: Do NOT position DevSignal as a "PLG CRM." That category is poisoned.**
